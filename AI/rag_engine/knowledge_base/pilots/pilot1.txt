Pilot Case Study: Community Solar Microgrid in Rural Tanzania

In 2019, a consortium of local NGOs, a solar energy company, and village cooperatives launched a pilot to provide reliable electricity to three villages in rural Tanzania. The villages had no grid connection and relied on kerosene and diesel generators. The pilot installed 120 kW of solar PV with battery storage, serving 450 households and 12 small businesses including grain mills and welding workshops.

The project used a pay-as-you-go (PAYG) model where households prepaid for energy via mobile money. Community members formed an energy cooperative that owned the infrastructure and hired local technicians for maintenance. Revenue covered operational costs within 18 months. Key outcomes included: 90% of households gained electric lighting; children's study hours increased by 2 hours per day; three new small enterprises opened; and diesel consumption dropped by 15,000 liters annually.

Challenges included initial resistance to prepayment, battery theft in one village, and limited technical capacity. Mitigations: intensive community sensitization, fenced and guarded battery enclosures, and partnership with a vocational school for technician training. The pilot was evaluated by an independent research institute. Key learnings: community ownership and transparent revenue sharing were critical for sustainability; PAYG models require reliable mobile money penetration; and women's inclusion in the cooperative improved repayment rates. The model has since been replicated in five additional villages.
